AI Literacy, Teachers, Education

AI Literacy in Education: Beyond Prompting

AI literacy is more than learning how to write better prompts. It is a broader educational competence that helps teachers understand, evaluate and use AI tools with professional judgement.

Artificial intelligence is increasingly present in education, but the conversation around it often begins and ends with tools. Teachers are shown how to use a chatbot, how to generate a worksheet, or how to write a more precise prompt. These skills can be useful, but they are not enough. AI literacy in education should be understood as a wider set of conceptual, practical, critical and ethical understandings.

At a conceptual level, teachers need to know what AI systems are and what they are not. They do not think, understand or care in the human sense. They generate outputs based on patterns in data. This distinction matters because it prevents unrealistic expectations and helps teachers explain AI more accurately to students.

At a practical level, AI can support aspects of teaching work. It may help teachers brainstorm lesson ideas, adapt texts for different levels, generate examples, plan speaking tasks or draft feedback. In language education, AI can also provide students with additional practice opportunities. However, these uses require careful review. AI-generated content can be inaccurate, biased, too generic or unsuitable for a particular group of learners.

Critical understanding is therefore essential. Teachers need to ask questions about reliability, bias, authorship, data protection and copyright. They also need to consider how AI may affect student learning. If a tool completes too much of the thinking process, students may produce work without developing the skills the task was designed to practise.

Ethical understanding is equally important. Schools need shared guidelines, not isolated individual use. Teachers should not be left alone to decide how AI fits into assessment, privacy, student data, academic honesty or classroom practice. A responsible school approach requires discussion, transparency and professional agreement.

AI literacy should also include students. Learners need to understand when AI can help, when it can mislead, and how to use it without replacing their own effort. This is especially important in writing, research and language learning, where the process is often more valuable than the final product.

Ultimately, teacher judgement remains central. AI may offer suggestions, but teachers know the students, the curriculum, the classroom context and the learning aims. The question is not whether AI is good or bad in general. The better question is: does this use of AI support meaningful learning in this specific context?

Moving beyond prompting means treating AI literacy as part of professional educational knowledge. It asks teachers and schools to combine curiosity with caution, experimentation with ethics, and innovation with pedagogy. That balance is where responsible educational use can begin.
